## Coaching depth score: 5/10
Structurally competent and well-organized, but it coaches the pitch as a writing exercise — it misses the sales-craft tells a seasoned manager would catch in the first listen.

## What's missing (the gaps a real coach would call out)

- **The miss:** Nobody flagged that "I run a *small* SaaS company" is a self-disqualifier. The coaching softens it to "a phrase a listener forgets" — that's the wrong diagnosis. The word "small" makes a buyer wonder if you'll be around in 18 months.
  - **What a coach would say:** "Cut the word 'small.' Just say 'I run TrackTide.' You're giving the buyer a reason to deprioritize you in your opening seven words."
  - **Why it matters:** Procurement and CS leaders kill deals over vendor viability. Jordan is handing them the objection for free.

- **The miss:** The coaching never questions whether "20% on average" is doing the work it should. A senior coach would push hard here — unattributed stats are wallpaper. Whose 20%? Over what period? Versus what baseline?
  - **What a coach would say:** "Replace 'about 20% on average' with a named proof point: 'Gainsight customer X cut churn from 8% to 6.4% in two quarters.' If you can't name the customer, name the segment and the timeframe. 'About 20% on average' is the stat buyers have learned to discount."
  - **Why it matters:** Right now the strongest line in the pitch is the weakest claim — soft number, soft hedge ("about"), no context. That's the line that should be the hammer.

- **The miss:** Zero discussion of who this pitch is *for*. The tool treats this as a one-size pitch that needs to be more specific — but a real coach asks: are you pitching the CS leader, the CRO, the founder, or the RevOps lead? Each one needs a different problem statement and a different CTA. The pitch is currently aimed at no one.
  - **What a coach would say:** "Before you fix any words, tell me who you're pitching. If it's the VP of CS, your hook is missed renewal targets and board questions. If it's the CRO, it's net revenue retention. You're trying to land one pitch on four buyers and it's landing on none."
  - **Why it matters:** This is the difference between a pitch that books meetings and a pitch that gets polite nods. Persona-less pitches are the #1 reason cold outreach fails.

- **The miss:** The CTA gets graded "exceeds" — that's generous to the point of misleading. "Would love to grab 15 minutes" is hedged language ("would love to," not "can we"), and "show you how it could work" is presumptive without having earned the right. A senior coach reads that as a rep who hasn't done discovery and is jumping to demo.
  - **What a coach would say:** "You haven't earned the demo yet. Try: 'Worth a 15-minute call next Tuesday or Thursday to see if churn visibility is even a priority for your team right now?' That's a qualifying close, not a pitching close."
  - **Why it matters:** Pitching-to-demo without qualification is how reps fill calendars with meetings that go nowhere. The current CTA optimizes for getting a yes, not for getting the right yes.

## What the coaching got right
- The structural map (identity / problem / solution / outcome / ask) is accurate and the tool correctly identifies that the pitch is generic.
- The "Tuesday or Thursday" suggestion on the CTA is a real, usable tactical fix.
- The "what does the CS leader do differently on Tuesday" framing under Value Prop is a genuinely good prompt — that's the one moment the coaching gets close to a real coach's instinct.

## The one cut
Cut "small" from the opener and replace "about 20% on average" with one named customer outcome — those two edits do more than every other note combined.
