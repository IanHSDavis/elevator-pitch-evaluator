# Critique: weak pitch (eval score 20/100)

**Pitch verdict from evaluator:** A product in search of a problem, a buyer, and an ask.

---

## Coaching depth score: 7/10
Solid structural coaching with real teeth in places, but it stays in pitch-coach lane and never reads the rep underneath the words.

## What's missing (the gaps a real coach would call out)

- **The miss:** No one named the obvious tell: this rep doesn't believe in their own pitch. "Anyway, I think you'd like it, you should check it out" is the verbal shrug of someone who's been brushed off before and is preempting it. The tool flagged the words ("apologizing for the pitch") but missed the state.
  - **What a coach would say:** "You're closing like someone expecting a no. 'Anyway' and 'I think you'd like it' are flinches — you're giving them the exit before they ask for one. The pitch isn't broken because you don't know your product; it's broken because you're hedging. Say the next version standing up."
  - **Why it matters:** Until the rep sees they're shrinking, they'll rewrite the words and still deliver them apologetically.

- **The miss:** "We just had a big launch and our traction is great" got zero coaching. That's a layup miss. Vague traction claims are worse than no traction claims — they signal the rep doesn't have real numbers to cite, or hasn't done the work to know which ones land.
  - **What a coach would say:** "Cut 'traction is great' entirely. Either you have a number — '40 hospital systems in 6 months,' 'cut denial resolution from 11 days to 6' — or you don't say it. Vague brag is a credibility leak; listeners assume the worst number you could've said."
  - **Why it matters:** Reps who learn to either cite or cut will sound 10x sharper across every pitch, not just this one.

- **The miss:** The tool gave the rep a menu of fixes ("try something like…", "or…") but never made the cut. Four sections, four "developing" ratings, four rewrites — the rep walks away with a rebuild project, not a next move. A senior coach picks the one thing.
  - **What a coach would say:** Pick the CTA or the problem statement and drill there. Everything else is downstream.
  - **Why it matters:** Reps fix one habit per coaching cycle. Four priorities = zero priorities.

- **The miss:** No sales vocabulary, no framing of who this pitch is even for. Is this a cold elevator moment with a stranger? A warm intro? An investor? The coaching pretends the context doesn't matter, but the right CTA for a prospect ("send you a Loom") is wrong for an investor and wrong again for a potential design partner. A real coach asks.
  - **What a coach would say:** "Who were you pitching? Because if that was a CIO at a conference, the Loom ask is wrong — you'd ask for 20 minutes with their revenue cycle lead. If it was an investor, none of this coaching applies; we'd rebuild around market and wedge."
  - **Why it matters:** Generic coaching produces generic reps.

## What the coaching got right
- The "founder-showing-off mode" diagnosis is accurate and named the right root cause.
- Calling out the vertical-list as a tell that the rep hasn't picked an ICP is sharp — that's a real pattern.
- The rewrite for the opener ("Priya, founder of Lumen…") models the move instead of just describing it. More coaching should do this.
- "Does anything means does nothing in particular" is a clean, memorable line the rep will actually remember.

## The one cut
Cut "anyway, I think you'd like it, you should check it out" and replace it with a direct ask — because the apology at the end is the symptom telling you the rep doesn't yet believe they've earned the conversation, and fixing that belief fixes the rest of the pitch.
