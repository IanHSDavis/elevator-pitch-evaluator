# Critique: strong pitch (eval score 100/100)

**Pitch verdict from evaluator:** A masterclass in pitch architecture — every beat lands.

---

## Coaching depth score: 4/10
Generic "exceeds" rubber-stamping that confuses a well-structured pitch with a strong one — a seasoned coach would have caught at least three tactical issues this tool celebrated.

## What's missing (the gaps a real coach would call out)

- **The miss:** Tool praises "compliance wedge" as "distinctive enough to stick." A real coach hears "wedge" and winces — it's VC/founder vocabulary, not buyer vocabulary. A CFO doesn't think of themselves as needing a "wedge"; that's how Maya thinks about her go-to-market, not how the buyer thinks about their problem.
  - **What a coach would say:** "Cut 'wedge.' Try: 'Lintel gets mid-market fintechs SOC 2 ready in ten days instead of eight weeks.' Lead with the outcome, not your category theory."
  - **Why it matters:** Maya is leaking founder-brain into a buyer conversation. Fixing this alone tightens every future pitch she gives.

- **The miss:** Tool completely misses that Maya is pitching the wrong persona at the wrong altitude. She opens by naming CFOs as the pain-bearer, then closes by asking to "walk your team through a live demo." Who is she actually pitching? If this is a CFO, a live demo is the wrong next step — CFOs don't sit through demos. If this is a champion below the CFO, the pain framing is misaligned. The tool calls the CTA "the model."
  - **What a coach would say:** "Who are you actually talking to here? If it's the CFO, kill the demo ask — they won't take it. Try: 'Can I send you a one-pager on how three Series-B fintechs hit audit-ready in ten days, and grab fifteen minutes if it's relevant?' If it's a Head of Eng or Compliance, then the demo works but rewrite the pain around their pager, not the CFO's runway."
  - **Why it matters:** This is the difference between a 15% and a 40% meeting-accept rate. Persona-CTA misalignment is the #1 reason qualified pitches die.

- **The miss:** "I know your team just closed a B round" — the tool calls this a "relevance hook that earns the ask." A senior coach hears this and thinks: how does Maya know? If it's public news, fine, but the line as delivered sounds like she's been LinkedIn-stalking. There's no "earning the right" — no referral, no trigger event tied to a named source, no prior touchpoint referenced.
  - **What a coach would say:** "Anchor that line. 'Saw the TechCrunch piece on your B round — congrats' lands very differently from 'I know your team just closed a B round,' which sounds surveilling. Name the source."
  - **Why it matters:** Cold outreach lives or dies on whether the prospect feels researched vs. scraped. One word fixes it.

- **The miss:** Tool offers no read on what's happening underneath the pitch. Maya has twelve customers and is pitching with the confidence of someone who's done this fifty times — that's actually a flag. She's pitching her deck, not running discovery. There's not a single question in this pitch. A real coach would notice she's monologuing at a prospect she could be qualifying.
  - **What a coach would say:** "You're pitching, not selling. Before the CTA, drop in: 'Most teams I talk to at your stage are either mid-audit or scoping one — where are you?' That one question turns this from a broadcast into a conversation and tells you in ten seconds whether Thursday is even worth it."
  - **Why it matters:** She'll qualify out bad-fit meetings before they hit her calendar and earn the right to a real conversation with the good-fit ones.

## What the coaching got right
- The note on "first twelve customers" inviting "were they all like me?" is a legitimate observation, and the suggested fix ("twelve Series-B fintechs") is the right instinct.
- Correctly identifies that the problem statement names persona, duration, and downstream consequence — that structural read is accurate.

## The one cut
Kill "wedge," kill the generic demo ask, and add one qualifying question before the CTA — you're broadcasting when you should be selling.
