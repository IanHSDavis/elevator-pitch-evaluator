# Critique: mid pitch (eval score 74/100)

**Pitch verdict from evaluator:** A clean, complete pitch that plays it safe at every turn.

---

## Coaching depth score: 5/10
Structurally competent and well-written, but it reads like a thoughtful pitch-coach checklist rather than a senior sales manager who's heard this exact pitch a hundred times.

## What's missing (the gaps a real coach would call out)

- **The miss:** The coaching never flags that "small SaaS company" is actively *working against* Jordan. It treats it as vague filler ("evaporates the moment it's said"). A real coach knows that when a founder volunteers "small," the buyer hears "risky vendor, might not be around in 18 months, junior team."
  - **What a coach would say:** "Cut the word 'small.' Full stop. You're telling a buyer to discount you before you've earned a sentence. Try: 'I'm Jordan, founder of TrackTide — we're the churn-intelligence layer for B2B SaaS.'"
  - **Why it matters:** Founders chronically under-sell themselves with self-deprecating qualifiers. Naming the pattern teaches Jordan to hear it everywhere — in email subject lines, on calls, in demos.

- **The miss:** No one called out that the 20% claim is unqualified and a sophisticated buyer will silently discount it. "About 20% on average" with no segment, no timeframe, no n= reads as marketing copy. The tool gestures at this but frames it as "push it further" rather than "this is a credibility risk."
  - **What a coach would say:** "Sharp buyers hear 'about 20% on average' and tune out — it sounds like a website stat. Either get specific ('our last six mid-market customers cut churn 18-24% in two quarters') or drop the number and tell one customer story instead. Vague proof is worse than no proof."
  - **Why it matters:** Jordan probably leans on this stat in every conversation. Reframing it as a liability rather than an asset changes how he opens every meeting.

- **The miss:** Zero discovery instinct in the CTA critique. The tool's fix — "Tuesday or Thursday?" — is calendar mechanics. The actual problem is that Jordan asked for a demo without earning the right to one. He has no idea if this person even owns churn as a problem.
  - **What a coach would say:** "You jumped from 'we exist' to 'book a demo' with no qualifying question. Replace the CTA with: 'Before I pitch you anything — is churn visibility actually a problem your team is wrestling with right now?' If yes, you've got a real meeting. If no, you just saved both of you 15 minutes."
  - **Why it matters:** This is the difference between a rep who books low-quality demos and one who builds pipeline. It's the single highest-leverage habit shift for a founder-seller.

- **The miss:** No read on the rep. This is a founder pitch — the energy is "I built a thing, please look at it" — and a senior coach would name that posture. The tool stays purely in word-level edits.
  - **What a coach would say:** "Right now you sound like a founder showing off the product. Your job in 30 seconds isn't to describe TrackTide — it's to make the buyer say 'wait, that's us.' Lead with their pain in their words, not your build."
  - **Why it matters:** Reframes the whole pitch from product-out to customer-in, which is the move most technical founders need to make and rarely get told directly.

## What the coaching got right
- The structural diagnosis is accurate — opening, problem, ask all generic; value prop is the strongest piece. That's the right read.
- The "named empathy lands harder than general empathy" line on the problem section is genuinely good coaching and uses concrete personas (CS leader in a QBR, CS ops manager).
- It correctly identified the value prop as load-bearing and told the rep to leave it alone — knowing what *not* to touch is a senior move and the tool got it right.

## The one cut
Drop "small" from the opener and replace the demo-ask with a qualifying question — you're discounting yourself and pitching before you've earned the right to.
