## Coaching depth score: 7/10
Better than typical tool output — it actually names tactical tells like "wedge" and "first twelve customers" — but it still hands the rep menus instead of cuts and misses a couple of things a seasoned coach would catch on first listen.

## What's missing (the gaps a real coach would call out)

- **The miss:** The coaching never flags that "the compliance wedge" is a *founder pitching an investor* opener, not a *vendor pitching a CFO* opener. It treats this as word choice ("wedge is GTM vocabulary") when it's actually a stance problem — Maya is in the wrong mode for the audience.
  - **What a coach would say:** "Maya, you're pitching this CFO like they're a seed investor. 'Compliance wedge,' 'first twelve customers,' 'mid-market fintechs preparing their first SOC 2' — that's your YC demo day deck. The CFO doesn't care about your category strategy or your customer count; they care whether their audit slips. Open with their problem, not your positioning: 'Most growth-stage fintech CFOs lose a quarter of runway to SOC 2 prep — we get you audit-ready in ten days.' Lead with the line you buried."
  - **Why it matters:** Reframes the entire pitch from founder-voice to buyer-voice in one move, which is the actual root cause of three of the four issues the tool flagged separately.

- **The miss:** The CTA critique gives the rep two fixes (cite the source AND add a qualifying question) and lets her pick. A senior coach makes the cut.
  - **What a coach would say:** "Cut 'I know your team just closed a B round' entirely. It's either creepy or unnecessary — if they did close, they know. Replace it with one qualifying question: 'Where are you on SOC 2 today — scoping, mid-prep, or hasn't started?' Their answer tells you if a demo is even the right next step, and it earns the ask."
  - **Why it matters:** One specific instruction beats a buffet. The rep walks away knowing exactly what to say Monday morning.

- **The miss:** No read on the rep. The pitch is technically perfect and emotionally airless — Maya is performing a pitch, not having a conversation. A senior coach hears that.
  - **What a coach would say:** "This is a recited pitch, not a conversation. Every beat is optimized, which is why it feels like a pitch. Slow down after the problem statement — give them a half-second to nod or wince. If you're talking for 35 straight seconds without a breath for them, you're monologuing, and CFOs tune out monologues."
  - **Why it matters:** The difference between a pitch that converts and one that gets a polite "send me materials" is whether the buyer felt heard, not whether the structure was clean.

- **The miss:** "Six weeks" in the problem and "eight weeks to ten days" in the value prop don't reconcile. The tool praised both sections without noticing the numbers don't line up — is the baseline six or eight weeks?
  - **What a coach would say:** "Pick one baseline number and use it twice. Right now you say CFOs spend six weeks chasing evidence, then claim you cut eight weeks to ten days. A sharp CFO catches that in real time and now they're doing math instead of listening."
  - **Why it matters:** Numerical precision is table stakes when you're selling to finance buyers. One inconsistency erodes the credibility the rest of the pitch built.

## What the coaching got right
- Caught "wedge" as GTM-vocabulary leaking into buyer conversation — that's a real tell most tools miss.
- Flagged "first twelve customers" as a risk signal to risk-averse buyers, with a usable rewrite.
- Correctly identified that "I know your team just closed a B round" reads as surveillance without a source.
- Named the right strategic gap: pitching where she should be qualifying.

## The one cut
Stop pitching the CFO like an investor — open with their pain in their language, and earn the demo by asking one qualifying question instead of asserting you know their roadmap.
