# Critique: mid pitch (eval score 82/100)

**Pitch verdict from evaluator:** Strong middle and close; the opening leaves memorability on the table.

---

## Coaching depth score: 6/10
Solid structure-level coaching with one genuinely sharp insight (the category line), but it reads like a thoughtful pitch consultant, not a sales manager who's been on 1,000 deals.

## What's missing (the gaps a real coach would call out)

- **The miss:** The coaching never names the elephant — Jordan opened by shrinking himself. "I run a small SaaS company" is a confidence tell, not a descriptor problem. The tool diagnosed it as "generic" and "unmemorable," which is the surface read.
  - **What a coach would say:** "Cut the word 'small.' You're discounting yourself before the buyer does. You're not a small SaaS company to them — you're the company that can save them 20% of their churn. Lead with that posture."
  - **Why it matters:** Reps who self-diminish in the opening get treated like vendors, not peers. This is a habit that bleeds into pricing conversations and CTAs later. Worth naming as a pattern, not a word choice.

- **The miss:** The CTA coaching tells Jordan to "qualify before you propose" but doesn't flag that the entire pitch has zero discovery in it. Jordan monologued for 45 seconds at a stranger and then asked for a meeting. A real coach would call that out as the core structural problem.
  - **What a coach would say:** "You pitched the whole product before you knew if churn is even a priority for them this quarter. Earn the right to pitch first: 'Before I tell you what we do — is reducing churn something your CS team is actively working on right now?' If yes, pitch. If no, you just saved both of you 30 seconds."
  - **Why it matters:** This is the difference between a rep who books meetings and a rep who books *qualified* meetings. The tool treated this as a polish item; it's the whole game.

- **The miss:** "20% on average" got a soft pass with a "make it harder to dismiss" note. A senior coach would push harder — that number is unsourced and lands like a marketing claim from the rep's own mouth.
  - **What a coach would say:** "Don't say '20% on average.' Say 'Gainsight customer in the mid-market cut churn from 14% to 11% in two quarters.' Specific customer, specific before/after, specific timeframe. Averages sound like brochure copy. Stories sound like proof."
  - **Why it matters:** Buyers discount averages by 50% automatically. Named proof points don't get discounted.

- **The miss:** No read on the rep. The coaching treats this as a writing exercise. A senior coach would notice Jordan's pitch is product-shaped end to end (I run, we built, it's helped, would love to) — five "I/we" sentences, zero questions, zero "you."
  - **What a coach would say:** "Count the I/we's versus the you's in your own pitch. You'll see the problem. You're so excited about what you built that you forgot the buyer is in the room."
  - **Why it matters:** This is the most common pattern in founder-led selling and the tool didn't pattern-match it.

## What the coaching got right

- The category-line rewrite ("the churn early-warning system for B2B SaaS CS teams") is genuinely good and exactly the kind of concrete substitution a senior coach would offer.
- The "named empathy beats general empathy" reframe with the QBR example is sharp and useable.
- It correctly identified the value prop as the strongest part and didn't try to fix what's working.
- It resisted the urge to pile on critique — the structure is restrained.

## The one cut
Stop pitching the product to strangers — open with one qualifying question so you find out whether churn is actually their problem before you spend your best lines on someone who doesn't have the pain.
