# Critique: weak pitch (eval score 20/100)

**Pitch verdict from evaluator:** "All product, no problem — and an ask that asks nothing."

---

## Coaching depth score: 6/10
Solid fundamentals coaching — names the right diagnoses and gives concrete rewrites — but it stays at the "fix the words" layer when a weak pitch like this needs a "fix the prep work" intervention.

## What's missing (the gaps a real coach would call out)

- **The miss:** The coaching keeps handing the rep finished sentences to mimic ("I'm [name], and we help hospital revenue teams..."). On a 20/100 pitch, the rep doesn't have a hospital revenue team story — they have nothing. Giving them polished example lines lets them swap in their own buzzwords and call it done.
  - **What a coach would say:** "Don't rewrite the pitch yet. Before you open your mouth again, go find the three customers you've actually helped, call them, and ask what their world looked like before you and after. You're pitching from imagination right now. That's the real problem — the words are downstream."
  - **Why it matters:** Reps in founder-mode don't have a wording problem, they have a discovery problem. Coaching them on phrasing reinforces the illusion that better words will fix it.

- **The miss:** Nobody named the "we just had a big launch and our traction is great" tell. That sentence is the loudest signal in the transcript — it's the rep trying to borrow authority because they can't generate it from customer outcomes. A senior coach catches that on first listen.
  - **What a coach would say:** "When you say 'our traction is great,' you're telling me you don't have a number you're proud enough to say out loud. If you had one, you'd lead with it. Either say '40 customers, $2M ARR, 3x in six months' or cut the line — vague traction claims actively lower trust with anyone sharp."
  - **Why it matters:** Hand-wave credibility signals are worse than no credibility signals; experienced buyers register them as a tell.

- **The miss:** The Value Prop coaching says "if you can't write that sentence yet, the pitch isn't ready" — and then moves on to the next dimension. That's the most important sentence in the entire coaching doc and it's buried as an aside. A real coach would stop the session there.
  - **What a coach would say:** "Stop. We're not doing the rest of this exercise until you can finish this sentence: 'Before us, [specific person] spent [time] doing [task] and got [bad outcome]. After us, they [new outcome].' If you can't fill that in for one real customer, you're not pitching, you're marketing — and you're bad at marketing. Go get the sentence. Then come back."
  - **Why it matters:** The whole pitch collapses to one missing artifact. Coaching that treats it as one of four equal weaknesses misses the leverage point.

- **The miss:** No coaching on context — who is the rep pitching to, in what setting, with what time? "Hey so uh" + 28 seconds reads like an elevator/networking attempt, not a meeting. The CTA fix ("send a two-minute Loom") may be wrong for the situation. Real coaches always ask "where are you saying this?" before fixing the words.
  - **What a coach would say:** "Walk me through where you actually use this. Is this a conference floor? An intro email read aloud? A first call? Because the right ask is completely different in each, and right now you're writing a pitch that fits nowhere."
  - **Why it matters:** Generic CTA advice produces generic CTAs. The setting determines the ask.

## What the coaching got right
- "Naming three industries is the opposite of naming a problem" — that's a real coach's line. Sharp, memorable, sticks.
- Calling out "I think you'd like it" as apologizing for the ask. That's a sales-craft observation, not a rubric observation.
- The "wallpaper" framing on innovative/cutting-edge is the right register — dismissive in the way a manager actually is about lazy language.

## The one cut
You don't have a pitch problem, you have a "haven't talked to your customers closely enough to steal their words yet" problem — go get one real before/after story from one real user, and the pitch will write itself.
