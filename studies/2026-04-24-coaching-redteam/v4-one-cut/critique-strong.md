# Critique: strong pitch (eval score 100/100)

**Pitch verdict from evaluator:** "A rubric-clean pitch — now sand off the founder-voice tells."

## Coaching depth score: 7/10
Solid senior-level coaching with two genuinely sharp catches ('wedge' as founder-speak, 'twelve customers' as stage-tell), but it misses a couple of tactical reads a 20-year coach would catch on first listen.

## What's missing (the gaps a real coach would call out)

- **The miss:** The coaching never flags that this pitch is aimed at the wrong buyer. Maya opens with "CFOs spend six weeks chasing engineers" — but CFOs don't chase engineers for audit evidence. Compliance leads, Heads of Security, or VP Eng do. CFOs *fund* the pain; they don't *feel* it daily. The evaluator praised the persona work as "textbook" when it's actually a persona mismatch.
  - **What a coach would say:** "Who actually lives this pain? A CFO signs the check, but it's the Head of Security or the compliance lead getting beat up by engineers. Decide which one you're pitching and rewrite the scene for them. If it's CFO, the pain is 'audit slips, B round slips' — lead with that, not the evidence-chasing. You're conflating economic buyer pain with end-user pain."
  - **Why it matters:** Pitching the wrong persona is the #1 reason demos that 'go great' never close. The rubric can't see it; a coach can.

- **The miss:** The coaching tells the rep to "drop the cohort size" but doesn't address the real problem — there's no named logo, no recognizable proof. For a CFO evaluating a compliance vendor (where vendor risk *is* the purchase), 'we cut time from 8 weeks to 10 days' without a name attached is a claim, not proof. The fix offered makes the line sound *better* but actually weaker.
  - **What a coach would say:** "Don't hide twelve — replace it. 'Ramp and Mercury cut their prep from eight weeks to ten days' beats any number-of-customers framing. If you can't name a logo, name the segment tightly: 'Series A fintechs running on AWS.' Specificity is the proof, not the metric."
  - **Why it matters:** In compliance and security sales, social proof IS the value prop. Coaching that polishes the wording without fixing the proof structure is cosmetic.

- **The miss:** The coaching gives the rep a menu ("try this... or consider that...") instead of making the cut. Three "try" suggestions, zero rewrites of the actual pitch. A senior coach hands the rep the new line.
  - **What a coach would say:** "Here's your new opener, say it back to me: 'Lintel is the compliance platform Series A fintechs use to get SOC 2 ready in ten days instead of eight weeks. Saw on TechCrunch you closed your B — compliance is usually next quarter's fire drill. Twenty minutes Thursday?' That's 25 seconds. Now you have 35 seconds to handle the first objection."
  - **Why it matters:** Coaching that doesn't produce a literal new script doesn't change Monday morning behavior.

- **The miss:** No read on delivery or pacing. 62 seconds with this much content means Maya is talking *fast*. A coach watching this live would clock that and say so.
  - **What a coach would say:** "You crammed five beats into 62 seconds. Cut a beat. Either the category line or the consequence line goes — pick one. Pitches that 'fit the window' by speeding up don't sell; they signal nerves."
  - **Why it matters:** The rubric measures content presence; buyers experience pacing.

## What the coaching got right
- Catching 'wedge' as founder-vocabulary leaking into buyer-vocabulary — that's the kind of ear you can't fake.
- Naming the 'I know your team just closed a B round' as surveilling-vs-researched, with the exact fix (cite the source).

## The one cut
Stop pitching the CFO with the security lead's pain — pick one buyer and rewrite the problem scene for the person who actually lives it.
