# Critique: strong pitch (eval score 100/100)

**Pitch verdict from evaluator:** A textbook pitch: specific opening, named pain, quantified outcome, sharp ask.

---

## Coaching depth score: 5/10
The coaching is articulate and well-organized, but it's a four-time victory lap on a pitch that has real tactical issues a seasoned coach would catch — it confuses "structurally clean" with "ready to run."

## What's missing (the gaps a real coach would call out)

- **The miss:** Coaching declared "exceeds" on every dimension and never asked the harder question: does this pitch actually qualify the buyer, or does it assume the pain? There's zero discovery built in. Maya is pitching at someone, not with them.
- **What a coach would say:** "You're product-pitching a stranger. Before the demo ask, insert one qualifier: 'Before I assume this is relevant — is SOC 2 prep actually owned in your org yet, or still unassigned?' If they say unassigned, you've found your champion problem. If they say it's owned, you now know who you actually need in the room on Thursday."
- **Why it matters:** Without this, Maya books demos with the wrong person and burns her best asset (the precision) on someone who can't buy.

- **The miss:** The coaching praised "I know your team just closed a B round and compliance prep is on next quarter's plan" without flagging the obvious problem: if it's on *next* quarter's plan, she's pitching too early and giving the prospect a built-in deferral ("great, circle back in 90 days"). A real coach catches this in their sleep.
- **What a coach would say:** "Your trigger line hands them the brush-off. Flip it: 'Most teams I talk to think compliance prep is a next-quarter problem until they realize evidence collection has a six-week tail — which means next quarter is actually this quarter.' Now you've reframed the timeline and earned the Thursday meeting."
- **Why it matters:** This is the difference between a booked demo and a "let's talk in Q3" that never happens.

- **The miss:** "We've cut time-to-audit-ready from eight weeks to ten days with our first twelve customers" — the coaching loved this. A senior coach would notice "first twelve customers" telegraphs early-stage and gives a risk-averse CFO a reason to wait. The number is a tell.
- **What a coach would say:** "Drop 'first.' Just say 'across twelve fintech deployments.' Same proof, none of the 'we're new' subtext. CFOs preparing for a Series B audit are not looking to be customer thirteen of an experiment."
- **Why it matters:** One word change, materially different risk posture in the buyer's head.

- **The miss:** Coaching never read Maya. Founder pitching her own company, leading with category-naming ("the compliance wedge") — this is classic founder-voice, clever for an investor audience, potentially too cute for a CFO who just wants the runway protected. No coach with a thousand pitches under their belt misses this pattern.
- **What a coach would say:** "'Compliance wedge' is a founder word, not a CFO word. Test it: does your best customer describe you that way, or do they say 'they got us audit-ready in two weeks'? Lead with how the buyer talks, not how you talk."
- **Why it matters:** Founders fall in love with their own positioning language; the rep needs to hear that the pitch that wins at demo day is not the pitch that wins a CFO.

## What the coaching got right
- Correctly identified that the CTA is specific and well-constructed (duration, window, format).
- The note about protecting the Series B linkage over the six-weeks number under compression is genuinely useful prioritization advice.
- Caught that the three-verb mechanism description is at the right length — that's a real observation.

## The one cut
"Next quarter's plan" is a deferral trap you handed them — reframe the timeline so Thursday becomes urgent, not optional.
